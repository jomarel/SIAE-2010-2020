Comparing organizational hierarchies

The contrast between centralized and decentralized governance anchors the paper. In Section 3.1, the insurer contracts separately with both the hospital and the physician, paying the hospital based on quantity and the physician on both quantity and intensity. In Section 3.2, the insurer contracts only with the hospital on both outputs, leaving the hospital to design the physician's contract.

Because this change alters the feasible contract space and reallocates residual risk rather than simply shifting authority, readers will find it challenging to interpret the comparisons in Section 4 and the Conclusion as effects of "organizational structure" or managerial autonomy. Furthermore, Figures 4–11 contrast the regimes almost exclusively through induced effort and output, though the objective functions formally include risk-sharing costs and participation rents. The model's comparative clarity would benefit from holding contractibility fixed across the settings, or from framing the exercise explicitly as direct versus delegated contracting while evaluating principal or total surplus.

Evaluating the perfect substitution limit

The paper highlights claims about performance when tasks are perfect substitutes, notably in the Abstract, Figure 3, and the Conclusion. A structural question arises regarding the algebraic evaluation of this boundary case. The analytical formulas for effort in Sections 3.1 and 3.2 require dividing by $\delta = \psi_1\psi_2 - \psi_{12}^2$.

Footnote 5 defines the perfect substitution scenario as $\psi_{12}^2 = \psi_1\psi_2$. At this exact boundary, the quadratic cost matrix becomes singular, meaning the displayed effort expressions are undefined. As a result, the claim that centralized contracting implies fixed physician pay under perfect substitution is not established by the currently displayed interior-point solutions. Readers need explicit regularity conditions delineating the admissible parameter space, alongside a dedicated limit analysis to maintain the boundary claims natively.

Generalizing the simulation findings

The numeric simulations in Section 4 are used to explore the model when the decentralized solution becomes too complex for a neat analytical comparison. The paper visualizes these patterns graphically, and footnote 10 specifies that all remaining parameters are set to one.

The Conclusion frames these visual findings—such as the decentralized "bias toward quantity contracting" or qualitative rankings as output signals become noisier—as structural properties of the model. Because only the special case $\psi_{12} = 0$ is solved analytically, readers cannot distinguish which findings are theorem-level implications from those that involve threshold phenomena or artifacts of the unit normalization. The presentation requires stronger analytical propositions for the key sign patterns, or a systematic analysis of the parameter regions to demonstrate where the simulation patterns broadly hold.

Aligning model motivation with formal results

The organizational framing of the paper draws on rich institutional details. The Introduction motivates the study with distinctions among Spanish public, private, and not-for-profit models, alongside considerations of managerial autonomy, physician salary practices, and altruism. Section 2.1 formalizes these vocational motives through the parameters $z(a)$ and $\phi(t_1, t_2)$.

As the formal analysis develops, the findings support narrower claims about contracting hierarchies under stylized multitask technologies. Section 3.1 establishes that under asymmetric information, the optimal payment scheme is identical for public and private organizations. Section 3.2 shows that altruistic parameters do not enter the payment components, and footnote 8 sets them to zero for the tractable decentralized case. The Conclusion nonetheless returns to ownership comparisons, proposes empirical tests across hospital types, and equates intensity with quality or performance, even though the formal structure lacks a mechanism to separate beneficial intensity from over-treatment. Anchoring the institutional ambitions in the Introduction and Conclusion directly to the structural mechanisms established in the algebra will provide stronger cohesion.

#1 Contradiction regarding zero incentives under perfect substitution Pending

Even more interestingly, although in the centralized structure all variable payments to physician tend to zero when tasks tend to perfect substitution, this is not the case in the decentralized one. As a consequence, when efforts become perfect substitutes it is optimal for the financer (the public provider or the insurance company) not to include incentives in the physician contract if the setup is centralized.

In Section 4.1, the text asserts that in the centralized structure, variable payments to the physician tend to zero when tasks tend toward perfect substitution (implying a fixed payment is optimal). However, for the unit parameter values utilized in the simulation, the formula for $B^C$ mathematically yields $1/3$ at $\psi_{12}=1$. This non-zero stabilizing value of approximately $0.33$ is precisely what is accurately illustrated in the right-hand side of Figure 3. The narrative appears to mistakenly abstract the theoretical limit for unmeasurable intensity (which rigidly requires $\sigma_2 \to \infty$, as cleanly noted in Section 3.1) into the standard simulation where $\sigma_2 = 1$.

#2 Sign error in hospital's fixed payment Pending

where $f=\bar{b}-(b-B)\left(a+t_{1}\right)-(d-D) t_{2}-z_{1} a-M$ is the fixed component of the hospital scheme that makes his partitipation constraint binding. The solution to this program is a complicate object that depends on the fundamental parameters.

In Section 3.2, the expression given for the fixed component $f$ appears to have a sign error for the total hospital cost $M$. The hospital's certainty equivalent is correctly formulated as subtracting costs $M$. Setting this certainty equivalent equal to the reservation utility $\bar{b}$ to define a binding participation constraint implies that $f = \bar{b} - (b-B)(a+t_1) - (d-D)t_2 - z_1 a + M$. The formula in the text subtracts $M$, which mathematically implies the fixed payment is reduced by the costs instead of exactly compensating for them.

The principal's optimization problem notation lists the realized random outputs $q$ and $i$ as choice variables under the maximization operator. To be strictly precise, the principal optimizes over the contract parameters ${f, b, F, B, D}$ along with the implicitly induced agent efforts. #6 Unnecessary overline notation in physician's FOCs Pending

$$ \begin{aligned} b^{C} & =\tau a-z_{1} \ B^{C} & =\psi_{1} t_{1}+\psi_{12} \bar{t}{2}-\phi{1} \ D^{C} & =\psi_{2} t_{2}+\psi_{12} \bar{t}{1}-\phi{2} \end{aligned} $$

In the physician's first-order conditions, the use of overlines on $t_1$ and $t_2$ (i.e., $\bar{t}_1$ and $\bar{t}_2$) is unneeded and slightly unorthodox. Since the physician behaves as a single agent simultaneously choosing both $t_1$ and $t_2$, neither action is taken as fixed or exogenous. Removing the overlines would make the notation fully consistent with the simultaneous algebraic solution shown in the subsequent step.
